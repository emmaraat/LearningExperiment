The effect of Spatial Frequency on Perceiving Abnormalities in Mammograms

The University of York would like to invite you to take part in the following research project.
Before agreeing to take part, please read this information sheet carefully and let us know if anything is unclear or you would like further information.

The study is designed to investigate the effect of spatial frequency on the perception of abnormalities in mammograms based on a short flash of the image.
You have been invited to take part because you are a specialist health practitioner (radiographer or radiologist) with formal qualification and/or experience in mammography image interpretation.

Instructions

In this experiment, we are asking you to do something you would never do in real practice:
We want you to make a decision about a pair of mammograms in a single glance.

On each trial, either a CC or MLO view of both breasts will be flashed on the screen.
You are asked to rate how certain you are that these images come from a woman with breast cancer, or who will develop it in the near future.
In 50% of these images, there is cancer or it will develop in the near future.
Some of the images you will see have been artificially modified.
Try to not let this influence your decision, focus only on your impression of the abnormality of the mammogram.
The flashes will be far too short to permit the usual scrutiny you would use,
but we want to know how much information you can gleam from a single glance.
Please give it your best shot!

To rate your certainty you will use your mouse to adjust the value of a slider on a 0-100 scale.
Make the rating based on your first impression:
0 means you are completely certain the images come from a healthy woman who will not develop breast cancer in the near future.
100 means you are completely certain the images come from a woman who has or will develop breast cancer in the near future.
Please adopt a liberal criterion, while being as accurate as possible.
To submit your rating, press the spacebar.
The next trial will start automatically after you press the space bar, so make sure you are ready and looking at the center of the screen.

The experiment will start with 3 practice trials. Then, you will do three experimental blocks of 120 trials each.
A typical session will last approximately 30 minutes.

Make sure you are in a quiet environment with minimal distractions and focus only on your screen.
Make sure you are in full screen mode, use F11 (windows) or Control + Command + F (mac) if you are not in full screen yet.

Press next when you have read and understood these instructions.
